Our Installation Process

Size the Space: Measure the entire measurement of all the linear walls in the area. For instance, you might need one 12-foot section of crown molding to cover each short wall, and a pair of 8-foot sections for each large wall, especially in a 165 sq. ft. room that has a couple of 15-foot walls and another two 11-foot walls.

Crown molding should be cut: To begin the process, the crown molding piece must be measured and indicated for cutting. While a manual miter saw is used for used for making miter cuts on crown molding, preferring a power miter saw can be preferred as cuts tend to be more exact.

Clean up the Wood: In many cases, most trim is already equipped with a primer coat of paint, but if you opt for bare wood molding, you should apply a coat of primer to the wood then allow it to dry for no less than an hour before attaching it onto the wall.

Locate and mark the wall studs: Use a stud finder and a pencil to identify and mark the wall studs on the wall surface, positioning the marks several inches beneath the junction of the wall and ceiling. These labels will serve as a point of reference when nailing the crown molding pieces together.

Fit the crown molding in place: Before attaching the crown molding on the wall, invert it ensuring that it is upright. Enlist an assistant to aid you in aligning the molding on a second ladder and controlling it as you proceed. Commence from the middle of the wall.

Caulk Holes: Gaps and spaces might sometimes appear along the molding and both the walls and ceiling, because infrequently are the walls and ceilings perfectly flat. To address these spaces, it's possible to fill them up by using a bead of flexible, paintable caulk along them. Any remaining gaps at the mitered corners are also rectified with caulk.

Painting the Molding: If you haven't already applied paint to them, put painter's tape along the upper and lower sections of the moldings at the points where they meet both the wall and ceiling.
