Our Installation Process

Size the Space: Measure the total length of all the linear walls in your space. To illustrate, you might need a single 12-foot section of crown molding to cover every short wall, and a pair of 8-foot sections for every large wall, especially in a 165 sq. ft. room with two 15-foot walls and another two 11-foot walls.

Crown molding should be cut: To begin the process, the crown molding piece needs to be measured and indicated for cutting. Even though a manual miter saw is used for employed for making miter cuts on crown molding, using a powered miter saw is preferred as cuts tend to be more precise.

Clean up the Wood: In many cases, most trim already comes featuring a paint primer, but if you opt for bare wood molding, you should use a coat of primer to the wood then let it cure for no less than an hour before installing it onto the wall.

Locate and mark the wall studs: Use a stud finder and a pencil to locate and label the wall studs on the wall surface, positioning the marks several inches beneath the wall/ceiling connection. These marks act as a point of reference while joining the crown molding pieces together.

Fit the crown molding in place: Preceding installing the crown molding to the wall, turn it over so that it is upright. Enlist an assistant to aid you in aligning the molding onto a second ladder and managing it as you proceed. Begin from the middle of the wall.

Caulk Holes: Gaps and spaces might periodically arise between the molding and the walls and ceiling, because rarely are the walls and ceilings absolutely flat. To address these spaces, you can seal them by applying a bead of flexible, paintable caulk along them. Any leftover gaps at those mitered corners are also filled with caulk.

Painting the Molding: If you haven't yet painted them, attach painter's tape at the top and bottom edges of the moldings at the points where they meet the wall and ceiling.
