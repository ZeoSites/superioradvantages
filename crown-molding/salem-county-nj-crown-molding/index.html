Our Installation Process

Size the Space: Measure the total length of all the linear walls in the area. For instance, it may require one 12-foot section of crown molding to trim every short wall, and a pair of 8-foot sections for every long wall, especially in a 165 sq. ft. area with a couple of 15-foot walls and two 11-foot walls.

Crown molding should be cut: To begin the process, the crown molding piece needs to be measured and indicated before cutting. Even though a manual miter box and saw can be used for making miter cuts on crown molding, using a powered miter saw is recommended since cuts will be more precise.

Clean up the Wood: In many cases, the majority of molding already comes with a paint primer, but if you happen to be using unpainted raw wood molding, you should apply a coat of primer to the wood then let it dry for no less than an hour before attaching it onto the wall.

Locate and mark the wall studs: Employ a stud finder and a pencil to identify and mark the wall studs on the wall surface, positioning the marks a few inches below the junction of the wall and ceiling. These marks act as a reference when nailing the crown molding pieces together.

Fit the crown molding in place: Preceding attaching the crown molding to the wall, turn it over ensuring that it is upright. Recruit an assistant to aid you in aligning the molding on a second ladder and controlling it as you proceed. Begin from the center of the wall.

Caulk Holes: Gaps might periodically arise along the molding and the walls and ceiling, as infrequently are the walls and ceilings perfectly even. To resolve these spaces, you can fill them up by using a line of flexible, paintable caulk along them. Any leftover gaps at those mitered corners can also be filled with caulk.

Painting the Molding: If you haven't yet painted them, put painter's tape along the upper and lower sections of the moldings where they intersect the wall and ceiling.
