Our Installation Process

Size the Space: Determine the entire measurement of the straight walls in your space. To illustrate, it may require one 12-foot section of crown molding to trim each short wall, and two 8-foot sections for each large wall, especially in a 165 square foot area with a couple of 15-foot walls and two 11-foot walls.

Crown molding should be cut: For the first step, the crown molding piece needs to be measured and marked before cutting. Even though a manual miter box and saw can be used for miter-cutting crown molding, preferring a power miter saw can be preferred as cuts will be more exact.

Clean up the Wood: In many cases, the majority of molding is already equipped with a paint primer, but if you opt for unpainted raw wood molding, you should apply a coat of primer to the wood then let it cure for no less than an hour before installing it to the wall.

Locate and mark the wall studs: Employ a stud finder and a pencil to locate and label each wall stud on the wall surface, positioning the marks a few inches below the junction of the wall and ceiling. These labels act as a point of reference when nailing the crown molding pieces together.

Fit the crown molding in place: Preceding installing the crown molding to the wall, turn it over ensuring that it is upright. Enlist an assistant to aid you in positioning the molding onto a second ladder and controlling it while you proceed. Begin at the center of the wall.

Caulk Holes: Gaps might sometimes appear between the molding and the walls and ceiling, because infrequently are these walls and ceilings absolutely even. To resolve these spaces, it's possible to seal them by applying a line of flexible, paintable caulk along them. Any remaining gaps at those mitered corners can also be filled with caulk.

Painting the Molding: If you haven't yet painted them, put painter's tape at the upper and lower sections of the moldings where they intersect the wall and ceiling.
