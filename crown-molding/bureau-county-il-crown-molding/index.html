Our Installation Process

Size the Space: Measure the total length of the straight walls in the area. To illustrate, it may require one 12-foot strip of crown molding to trim each short wall, and two 8-foot sections for each large wall, especially in a 165 sq. ft. area with two 15-foot walls and two 11-foot walls.

Crown molding should be cut: To begin the process, the crown molding piece must be measured and indicated for cutting. While a manual miter saw can be used for miter-cutting crown molding, using a power miter saw can be recommended as the cuts will be more exact.

Clean up the Wood: Usually, most trim is already equipped with a paint primer, but if you happen to be using unpainted raw wood molding, you should use a coat of primer to the wood then let it dry for no less than an hour before installing it onto the wall.

Locate and mark the wall studs: Use a stud finder and a pencil to locate and label each wall stud on the wall surface, positioning the marks several inches beneath the wall/ceiling connection. These labels act as a point of reference while nailing the crown molding pieces together.

Fit the crown molding in place: Preceding installing the crown molding to the wall, invert it so that it is in an upright position. Recruit an assistant to aid you in aligning the molding on a second ladder and controlling it while you proceed. Commence at the middle of the wall.

Caulk Holes: Gaps and spaces may periodically arise between the molding and the walls and ceiling, as infrequently are these walls and ceilings perfectly even. To resolve these spaces, you can seal them by using a bead of flexible, paintable caulk over them. Any remaining gaps at those mitered corners are also filled with caulk.

Painting the Molding: If you haven't yet painted them, put painter's tape along the upper and lower sections of the moldings at the points where they meet the wall and ceiling.
