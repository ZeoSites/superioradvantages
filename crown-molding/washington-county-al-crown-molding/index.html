Our Installation Process

Size the Space: Determine the entire measurement of the straight walls in your space. For instance, you might need one 12-foot section of crown molding to trim each short wall, and two 8-foot sections for each large wall, especially in a 165 square foot room that has a couple of 15-foot walls and two 11-foot walls.

Crown molding should be cut: For the first step, the crown molding piece needs to be measured and indicated before cutting. While a manual miter saw is used for used for making miter cuts on crown molding, using a powered miter saw is recommended since the cuts will be more precise.

Clean up the Wood: Usually, most trim already comes with a primer coat of paint, but if you opt for bare wood molding, it's advisable to use a coat of primer to the wood then allow it to dry for no less than an hour before attaching it onto the wall.

Locate and mark the wall studs: Use a stud finder and a pencil to identify and mark each wall stud on the wall surface, positioning the marks several inches beneath the junction of the wall and ceiling. These marks will serve as a reference when joining the crown molding pieces together.

Fit the crown molding in place: Before attaching the crown molding on the wall, turn it over ensuring that it is upright. Enlist an assistant to aid you in aligning the molding on a second ladder and controlling it while you proceed. Begin from the middle of the wall.

Caulk Holes: Gaps and spaces might sometimes appear along the molding and both the walls and ceiling, as infrequently are the walls and ceilings perfectly even. To resolve these spaces, it's possible to fill them up by using a bead of flexible, paintable caulk over them. Any leftover gaps at the mitered corners can also be filled with caulk.

Painting the Molding: If you haven't yet painted them, attach painter's tape along the top and bottom edges of the moldings at the points where they meet both the wall and ceiling.
