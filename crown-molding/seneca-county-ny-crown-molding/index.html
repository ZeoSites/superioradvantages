Our Installation Process

Size the Space: Measure the entire measurement of the straight walls in your space. For instance, you might need one 12-foot strip of crown molding to trim every lesser wall, and a pair of 8-foot sections for every large wall, especially in a 165 sq. ft. area with two 15-foot walls and another two 11-foot walls.

Crown molding should be cut: For the first step, the crown molding piece must be measured and indicated before cutting. While a manual miter saw can be employed for miter-cutting crown molding, preferring a powered miter saw can be recommended as cuts tend to be more exact.

Clean up the Wood: In many cases, the majority of molding already comes with a paint primer, but if you opt for unpainted raw wood molding, it's advisable to use a coat of primer to the wood and let it cure for at least an hour before installing it onto the wall.

Locate and mark the wall studs: Use a stud finder and a pencil to identify and mark each wall stud on the wall surface, positioning the marks a few inches below the junction of the wall and ceiling. These marks will serve as a point of reference while joining the crown molding pieces together.

Fit the crown molding in place: Before installing the crown molding to the wall, turn it over so that it is in an upright position. Recruit an assistant to help you in positioning the molding on a second ladder and controlling it while you start. Begin at the middle of the wall.

Caulk Holes: Gaps might sometimes appear along the molding and the walls and ceiling, as infrequently are the walls and ceilings perfectly even. To resolve these spaces, it's possible to fill them up by applying a line of flexible, paintable caulk along them. Any remaining gaps at those mitered corners can also be filled with caulk.

Painting the Molding: If you haven't yet painted them, put painter's tape at the upper and lower sections of the moldings at the points where they meet the wall and ceiling.

Seneca County is located in the U.S. state of New York. As of the 2020 census, the population was 33,814. The primary county seat is Waterloo, moved there from the original county seat of Ovid in 1819. It became a two-shire county in 1822, which currently remains in effect and uses both locations as county seats although the majority of Seneca County administrative offices are located in Waterloo. Therefore, most political sources list only Waterloo as the county seat. The county's name comes from the Seneca Nation of the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois), who occupied part of the region.
